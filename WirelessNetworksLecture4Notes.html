01

Wireless is a Shared Medium

Slide 3 — The foundational distinction that drives all of MAC

🔌 Wired Networks: Independent Links

In a wired network, each communication pair has a dedicated physical wire connecting them. Alice and Bob's traffic flows through their wire; Cathy and Eve's traffic flows through a completely separate wire. There is no physical overlap. The medium is not shared — each link is point-to-point, private, and isolated.

This means a station can always "listen while it talks" (the signal on the wire is deterministic) and can detect if another station's signal adds to the wire's voltage — this is exactly how CSMA/CD (Collision Detection) in Ethernet works.

📡 Wireless Networks: Broadcast Medium

Radio waves expand outward in all directions from an antenna. There is no wire to contain them. Every station within radio range receives every transmission. When two nearby stations transmit at the same time on the same frequency, their electromagnetic waves literally overlap and interfere at any receiver in range.

The critical phrase from the slide is: "Close by wireless conversations share the same wireless medium." Proximity is the determining factor — not logical connection.

Left: wired links are fully independent. Right: wireless signals broadcast to all nearby stations — creating potential collisions.
Professor's Picky Corner
Can a wired network ever have a "collision"? Yes — in old hub-based Ethernet (not modern switches). Hubs broadcast to all ports, making it effectively a shared medium. But individual wire-pair links to a switch are point-to-point. Know this distinction.
The slide says "close by" — not all wireless stations share the medium. Two stations on opposite sides of a city on the same channel don't interfere because the signal attenuates to noise by then. Proximity is the key constraint.
Wireless is called a "broadcast medium." This is not the same as IP broadcast (sending to 255.255.255.255). It is a physical-layer property — every transmission is physically broadcast to all nearby receivers whether you want it or not.
02

CSMA: Carrier Sense, Multiple Access

Slide 4 — The basic protocol and the core problem it cannot fully solve

🔍 Unpacking the Acronym

CSMA is not one protocol — it is a family of protocols built on two core ideas. Understanding each word is essential:

Multiple Access (MA)
The channel is a shared medium. Multiple stations — each being either a wireless host (laptop, phone) or an access point (AP) — want to use the same channel simultaneously. There is no central scheduler telling anyone when to talk.
Carrier Sense (CS)
Before transmitting, a station "listens" to the channel. It tries to detect whether another station is currently transmitting ("carrying" a signal). If the channel is busy, the station defers its transmission to prevent a collision. This is the "listen before you talk" principle.

✅ Why CS Helps

Carrier sense prevents a station from knowingly starting a transmission when the channel is occupied. If A is transmitting to B, and C senses this, C waits until A finishes. This dramatically reduces collisions compared to pure random access (ALOHA) where stations transmit without listening at all.

⚠️ The Core Limitation in Wireless

The slide states the key caveat: "detecting ongoing transfers isn't always possible." In wireless, due to signal attenuation and the hidden terminal problem (explained later), a station may genuinely be unable to hear another station's transmission even though they will both interfere at the receiver. CS cannot catch what it cannot hear.

Signal attenuation: A can communicate with B, and C can communicate with B, but A and C may be mutually inaudible — leading to undetected concurrent transmissions.
Professor's Picky Corner
The slide says "station: wireless host or access point." A station is NOT just a laptop. An AP is also a station participating in CSMA. This matters for exams: the AP must also follow carrier sense rules, it is not automatically privileged.
The signal strength diagram in Slide 4 shows A and C on opposite ends with B in the middle. A's signal is strong where A is and decays as you move toward C. C's signal is strong at C. They may not overlap at each other's location — meaning CS is ineffective across this distance. This diagram WILL generate exam questions about what each node can sense and when.
03

Collision Detection vs. Collision Avoidance

Slides 5, 6, 8 — The wired strategy and why it fails wirelessly

🔋 CSMA/CD (Collision Detection) — Wired Ethernet Strategy

CSMA/CD is the classic wired Ethernet MAC protocol. Its procedure is:

1
Listen (Carrier Sense) Station waits until the medium appears idle — no one else's signal is detected on the wire.
2
Start Transmitting — and Keep Listening The station begins its frame transmission but simultaneously listens to the wire. In a wired medium, the transmitter can read back what is on the wire while it writes.
3
Detect Collision If another station also starts transmitting, both signals add up on the wire. The voltage level on the wire deviates from what each sender alone would produce. The sender detects this discrepancy — it senses a collision.
4
Abort and Backoff On collision detection, the station immediately aborts its transmission (sending a jamming signal to alert others), waits a random backoff time (exponential backoff), then tries again from Step 1.
Why CD is Efficient

CD is more efficient than just CA because it aborts on collision. Without CD, a station would transmit its entire frame only to discover at the end (via missing ACK) that the transmission was corrupted. With CD, it stops the moment a collision is detected — wasting only the propagation delay worth of airtime, not the full frame duration.

🚫 CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance) — Wireless Strategy

Since wireless cannot do collision detection (see Section 5 for the deep explanation), 802.11 Wi-Fi uses collision avoidance instead:

1
Listen and Wait Until Free Station performs carrier sense and waits until no transmission is detected.
2
Wait a Random Backoff Time Even after the medium becomes free, the station waits an additional random duration before transmitting. This random spread reduces the probability that two stations who were both waiting for the channel to free up will transmit simultaneously.
3
Transmit — No Abort on Collision Station transmits the full frame. If a collision happens, it cannot detect it mid-flight. It discovers the loss only via the absence of an ACK.
✓ Advantage

Simple to implement in hardware. No need for simultaneous transmit+receive circuitry. Reduces collisions proactively rather than reactively.

✗ Disadvantage

Cannot recover from a collision mid-flight. The full frame is always transmitted to completion even if it's going to be garbage. Recovery happens only after waiting for an ACK timeout.

Professor's Picky Corner
The slide says CD is "Used for hub-based Ethernet." Modern switched Ethernet does NOT use CSMA/CD because switch ports are full-duplex point-to-point links — collisions literally cannot happen. If asked "does modern Gigabit Ethernet use CSMA/CD?" the answer is NO.
CA does not eliminate collisions — it reduces them. Two stations can still both sense an idle channel simultaneously (between each other's check and their actual transmission), decide to transmit, and collide. The random backoff is probabilistic, not a guarantee.
"Collision avoidance" in CSMA/CA refers to the random backoff before transmitting, not to RTS/CTS (which provides a different kind of avoidance). CSMA/CA + RTS/CTS is one complete system, but the CA part specifically is the backoff-before-transmit.
04

Wireless Interference

Slide 7 — What actually happens physically when two stations transmit simultaneously

💥 The Physics of Collision: Signal Superposition

When two radio transmitters operate on the same frequency at the same time and their signals overlap at a receiver's location, the receiver experiences the superposition (mathematical addition) of both electromagnetic waves. This is not a digital phenomenon — it is pure physics.

Each transmitter sends a carefully structured waveform that encodes bits. At the receiver, the radio demodulation circuit must decode this waveform back into bits. When two independent, unrelated waveforms are added together, the result is neither waveform — it is a corrupted, unintelligible signal. The slide says: "Receiver gets garbage."

🧮 Mathematical Model of Collision
received(t) = signal_A(t) + signal_C(t) + noise(t)
1
signal_A(t) encodes A's bits as, say, BPSK or QAM modulation on carrier frequency f₀
2
signal_C(t) encodes C's bits independently on the same carrier f₀
3
The receiver's demodulator sees a linear combination it was not designed for. It cannot separate A's bits from C's bits — it would need MIMO/SIC capabilities not present in basic 802.11
Result: Bit Error Rate → ~50% (essentially random guessing). The frame is discarded.

The slide illustrates this with the classic A-B-C triangle: A transmits to C, B transmits to C simultaneously. C is the receiver that gets "garbage" from the combined signal. Both A's and B's transmissions are wasted.

📶 Other Sources of Interference (Slide 10)

Collisions from other wireless stations are not the only interference problem. The lecture notes that the 2.4 GHz ISM band is shared with many non-Wi-Fi devices:

  • Microwave ovens: Operate around 2.45 GHz and leak significant RF energy, causing bursty interference during operation.
  • Bluetooth devices: Frequency-hop across the 2.4 GHz band. When a Bluetooth hop lands on the same channel as Wi-Fi, it causes interference.
  • Baby monitors, cordless phones: Older 2.4 GHz models transmit continuously on fixed channels.
  • Motors and other electronics: Create broadband electromagnetic noise across many frequencies.
Key Point

The 5 GHz and 6 GHz bands (used by 802.11a/n/ac/ax) have less interference from non-Wi-Fi devices precisely because fewer household appliances use those frequencies. This is one reason 5 GHz Wi-Fi often performs better in congested environments.

Professor's Picky Corner
The slide (Slide 7) shows the collision at C, not at A or B. This is because collisions are events that happen at the RECEIVER. The transmitters A and B may have no idea a collision occurred. This is a crucial insight the lecture returns to repeatedly.
Interference is asymmetric in its consequences: the transmitters' frames are both wasted, but neither transmitter automatically knows this (without CD or ACK feedback). This asymmetry drives the need for link-layer ACKs.

Why Wireless Cannot Do Collision Detection

Slides 5, 8, 12, 13 — Two fundamental problems that make CD infeasible

🚩 Problem #1: Cannot Detect All Collisions

Even if a transmitter tried to listen while transmitting, it still cannot detect all collisions because:

The Hidden Terminal Problem

Node A is transmitting to B. Node C also wants to transmit to B. A performs carrier sense — but A cannot hear C's signal (they are too far apart, or there is an obstacle between them). A senses an idle channel and transmits. C also senses idle and transmits simultaneously. Result: collision at B. Neither A nor C detected the other's carrier.

Fading

Even when two nodes are mutually visible, the signal between them can fade to below the carrier-sense threshold due to multipath destructive interference. A sender may falsely believe the channel is idle when another station is transmitting but its signal happens to be in a destructive fade at the listener's location.

🔧 Problem #2: Listening While Sending is Impractical

Even setting aside the hidden terminal problem, there is a fundamental hardware problem with listening while transmitting in wireless:

Signal Strength Mismatch

When a wireless transmitter sends, its own signal at its own antenna is on the order of 100–500 mW (20–27 dBm). Another station's signal arriving from, say, 50 meters away might be −70 dBm (0.1 nanowatts). That is a ratio of roughly 10⁹ : 1 (90 dB difference). The transmitter's own signal would completely overwhelm and mask any incoming signal from a colliding station.

🧮 The Signal Strength Problem
Tx Power ≈ +20 dBm = 100 mW
→
Received signal from nearby colliding transmitter ≈ −70 dBm = 0.0001 mW
→
Ratio = 100 / 0.0001 = 10⁶ (60 dB) to 10⁹ (90 dB) difference
Self-interference masks the collision signal. The receiver cannot "see through" its own transmission.

Hardware Cost

To listen while transmitting, the radio would need a second receiving antenna and a very expensive RF isolation circuit (a circulator or duplexer) that can cleanly separate its own transmitted signal from the received signal on the same or adjacent frequency. This circuit exists in full-duplex cellular base stations (at high cost), but the slide notes it is "expensive to build hardware that detects collisions" — too expensive for consumer Wi-Fi chipsets.

The conclusion from the slides is explicit: "So, 802.11 does NOT do collision detection."

Professor's Picky Corner
The slide says "Radios are not full duplex." This is a KEY point. Full-duplex radio (transmitting and receiving simultaneously on the same frequency) is technically possible with self-interference cancellation (used in research and some newer Wi-Fi 6/7), but standard 802.11 is half-duplex. A station is either transmitting OR receiving at any given moment — never both at once on the same channel.
The two problems are independent — you need to be able to explain both separately. Problem 1 (hidden terminal, fading) means CD would miss collisions even if you could do it. Problem 2 (signal strength, hardware cost) means you physically cannot implement CD on standard hardware. Together they make CD doubly infeasible.
The phrase "high loss rates" in slide 13 as a consequence of "no fate sharing" is particularly examinable. High loss rates in wireless come from BOTH collisions (MAC problem) AND channel impairments (physical layer). CD cannot solve channel impairments — MAC can only address collisions.

Fate Sharing & Signal Attenuation

Slides 9, 11, 12, 13 — The wired vs. wireless comparison and the critical "No Fate Sharing" concept

📐 "Fate Sharing" in Wired Networks

In a wired network (slide 9), when A and C both transmit and the collision occurs at receiver B (the midpoint on the wire), the signal state at transmitter A's location is almost identical to the signal state at receiver B's location. This is because the wire is a guided medium — the same voltages/currents propagate with very little loss along the conductor.

The slide calls this "Fate sharing of the link." Because A and B experience essentially the same signal environment, A can infer what B is experiencing by reading back its own wire. If A's signal is mixed with C's signal at A's own antenna (via the wire connection), A knows B is also experiencing that mixture.

Why This Enables CD in Wired Networks

Fate sharing means the transmitter (A) can detect the collision by monitoring the wire it's connected to with a second receive antenna. This is exactly what CSMA/CD Ethernet does — listen to the wire while transmitting and abort if the received signal doesn't match what was sent.

📉 "No Fate Sharing" in Wireless Networks

In a wireless network (slide 12), the signal state at transmitter A's location is NOT identical to the signal state at receiver B's location. Why? Because radio signals decay with distance and obstacles. The signal from C might be very strong at B (because C is near B) but extremely weak or undetectable at A (because A is far from C).

The slide explicitly states: "'No Fate sharing' of the link" — meaning A cannot infer what B is experiencing by monitoring its own local radio environment.

📊 Attenuation and Propagation Range (Slide 11)

The attenuation diagram from slide 11 shows the relationship between signal power and distance:

  • Transmitted power starts high at the transmitter.
  • Propagation loss causes the signal to decay roughly as 1/d² (free-space) or faster with obstacles — this is path loss.
  • SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio) = Signal power / Noise floor. The signal must remain above the noise floor for reception.
  • Sensitivity is the minimum signal level the receiver's hardware can decode.
  • Maximum range is the distance at which SNR drops below the minimum required value (minimum SNR). Beyond this, communication fails.
🧮 Path Loss Model (Free Space)
PL(d) = 20·log₁₀(d) + 20·log₁₀(f) + 20·log₁₀(4π/c) [dB]
d
distance from transmitter in meters
f
frequency in Hz (e.g., 2.4 GHz = 2.4 × 10⁹ Hz)
c
speed of light ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s
SNR at receiver = Tx_Power(dBm) - PL(d)(dB) - Noise_Floor(dBm). Reception requires SNR ≥ SNR_min.
Professor's Picky Corner
The attenuation graph (Slide 11) labels "Maximum attenuation" on the right end. This is the maximum path loss the link can tolerate while maintaining minimum SNR. It equals: Tx_Power - Rx_Sensitivity. This is a direct link budget calculation — your professor may ask you to compute maximum range given transmit power and sensitivity.
The term "minimum SNR" on the graph is the SNR threshold below which the modulation/coding scheme cannot decode reliably. This threshold depends on the modulation: BPSK requires lower SNR than 64-QAM for the same error rate. This links to adaptive modulation rate control in Wi-Fi (MCS selection).
"Variable channel conditions" from slide 13 is a second reason for "no fate sharing." Even if distance were identical, multipath fading means the channel can be in a deep fade at one location but strong at another nearby location. This randomness makes CD unreliable even in theory.

Link-Layer Acknowledgments as the Solution

Slide 14 — How 802.11 detects packet loss without collision detection

📬 The ACK Mechanism

Since wireless cannot do collision detection, 802.11 needs another mechanism to know when a frame was lost. The solution is mandatory link-layer acknowledgments (ACKs):

  • After every unicast data frame, the receiver is required to send an ACK frame to the sender within a very short, fixed time called SIFS (Short Inter-Frame Space, typically 16 µs for 802.11g).
  • The sender starts a timer after transmitting. If no ACK arrives before the timer expires, the sender assumes the frame was lost — either due to a collision or channel error.
  • The sender then retransmits the frame after a random backoff.

The transmission sequence shown in the slide is:

DIFS
→
Data Tx
→
SIFS
→
ACK Tx
→
Random Backoff
DIFS — DCF Inter-Frame Space
The mandatory wait time a station must observe after sensing the channel idle before beginning a new data transmission or after a collision-induced backoff. Longer than SIFS, which ensures that ACKs (sent after SIFS) always get priority over new transmissions (sent after DIFS). Typical value: 34 µs for 802.11g.
SIFS — Short Inter-Frame Space
A shorter inter-frame gap used by the receiver to respond with an ACK (or CTS in RTS/CTS exchanges). Its shorter duration ensures that the ACK/CTS frame gets channel priority over any other station that might start transmitting. This prevents a third party from "cutting in" between the data frame and its ACK.

⚠️ The Fundamental Limitation of ACKs Alone

The slide explicitly notes a critical problem: "Does not handle hidden terminal cases."

Why? In the hidden terminal scenario (A transmits to B, C also transmits to B because C cannot hear A), the collision happens at B. B receives a corrupted frame and sends no ACK. Both A and C experience ACK timeouts and retransmit — potentially colliding again. The ACK mechanism tells the transmitter a collision happened but provides no mechanism to prevent it from happening again. That's what RTS/CTS solves.

Important: Link-layer ACKs are a detection mechanism (detect loss after the fact). RTS/CTS is a prevention mechanism (prevent hidden terminal collisions before they occur). Both are needed in 802.11.
Professor's Picky Corner
DIFS > SIFS (DIFS is longer). This ordering is intentional and examinable. The shorter SIFS guarantees that ACK frames always have priority and get sent before any station waiting with DIFS can start a new transmission. If SIFS > DIFS, ACKs could be delayed by new transmissions, breaking the protocol.
ACKs are sent only for unicast frames. Broadcast/multicast frames in 802.11 are not acknowledged. This is why multicast Wi-Fi is unreliable — there is no retransmission mechanism. Your professor can ask: "Why is wireless multicast unreliable at Layer 2?"
The "Random Backoff" after the ACK (shown in the timeline) is for the next frame. After receiving an ACK confirming successful delivery, the station must wait DIFS + random backoff before transmitting the next data frame. It cannot immediately grab the channel again.

The MAC Problem Defined

Slides 19, 20 — The formal problem statement that every MAC protocol tries to solve

🎯 The Core Problem Statement

The slide states: "Receiver must hear ≤ 1 strong transmission at a time, else collision (transmissions are lost)."

This single constraint drives the entire field of MAC protocol design. A receiver can only decode one transmission at a time (without MIMO/SIC capabilities). If two signals of comparable strength overlap at a receiver, neither can be decoded.

The MAC problem then breaks into two sub-questions:

Question 1: How to Divide the Medium
The shared medium has two dimensions: time (when) and frequency (which band). A MAC protocol must decide how to carve up these dimensions to give different stations access without simultaneous interference at receivers.
Question 2: Who Gets What, and When
Once the division scheme is chosen (e.g., time slots, frequency bands, or codes), the protocol must determine the assignment: which station gets which slot, which band, which code — and at what time.
The 2D Resource View

The slide shows the channel as a 2D space: frequency on the Y-axis, time on the X-axis. Different MAC protocols carve this space differently. FDMA slices horizontally (each user gets a frequency band across all time). TDMA slices vertically (each user gets a time slot across all frequencies). CDMA overlays (users share all time and all frequency but use orthogonal codes to stay separate).

Professor's Picky Corner
The slide uses the phrase "strong transmission" — ≤1 strong transmission. This implies that a very weak second signal might not cause a collision if it is far below the strong signal. This concept is called the "capture effect" — a receiver can "capture" the strongest signal if it is sufficiently stronger than any interferer. Some advanced receivers exploit this, but basic 802.11 does not.
A "MAC protocol" specifies both questions. It is not just an allocation rule — it must also specify the mechanism for knowing who gets which resource. You must be able to explain both halves for any MAC protocol (TDMA, FDMA, CSMA) on the exam.

TDMA: Time Division Multiple Access

Slide 22 — Dividing the channel in the time dimension

⏱️ How TDMA Works

TDMA divides the channel's time axis into a repeating sequence of fixed-duration frames (rounds). Within each frame, there are N fixed-duration time slots — one pre-assigned to each user. A station may only transmit during its designated slot.

  • During its slot, the station uses the full bandwidth of the channel for that fixed duration.
  • Slots repeat cyclically: station 1 → slot 1, station 2 → slot 2, … station N → slot N, then back to station 1.
  • Assignment of slots to stations is done out-of-band — via a separate signaling channel or control protocol.

The Critical Insight

Even though stations 2, 5, and 6 are idle, their slots remain empty in every round. No other station can use them. This is the fundamental inefficiency of provisioned protocols: resource waste when the system is under-loaded.

🧮 TDMA Capacity Analysis
Max throughput per user = R / N
R
Total channel data rate (e.g., 54 Mbps)
N
Number of slots per round (= total users provisioned for)
→
With N=6 stations and R=54 Mbps, each gets exactly 9 Mbps maximum, always, even when others are idle
Actual channel utilization = (number of active stations / N) × 100%. With 3 of 6 active: 50% channel utilization.
Professor's Picky Corner
The slide says "send across all frequencies during this slot." TDMA slots use the full channel bandwidth during their slot. This is different from FDMA where each user is always restricted to a narrow frequency band. TDMA gives temporal exclusivity but spatial/frequency inclusivity.
Real-world TDMA: GSM cellular networks use TDMA. Each base station frequency has 8 TDMA slots of ~577 µs each, repeating every ~4.6 ms. Active calls get one slot per frame — that's why early 2G phones caused audible clicking in nearby speakers.
The slide's example uses exactly 6 slots for 6 stations. The exam may ask: if there are only 3 active stations in a 6-slot TDMA system, what is the effective throughput per station? Answer: still 1/6 of channel capacity per station (slots cannot be reassigned dynamically), though channel utilization is only 50%.

FDMA: Frequency Division Multiple Access

Slide 23 — Dividing the channel in the frequency dimension

📻 How FDMA Works

FDMA divides the channel's total frequency range into N fixed sub-bands (frequency channels), one per user. Each user transmits continuously on their assigned frequency band — across all time.

  • A station's frequency band is exclusively theirs — they can transmit whenever they have data, without coordinating with others on different bands.
  • The sub-band width limits each user's data rate: narrower band = lower throughput (Nyquist/Shannon limit).
  • Guard bands — small unused frequency gaps between adjacent bands — prevent adjacent channel interference. These guard bands are wasted spectrum.

Real-World FDMA

Early analog cellular (AMPS/1G) used FDMA — each call got a 30 kHz frequency band. FM radio is also FDMA — each station occupies a specific frequency range (e.g., 98.5 MHz). Cable TV traditionally used FDMA — each TV channel on a separate frequency.

OFDMA (Modern Evolution)

Modern 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6) uses OFDMA — Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access. Instead of fixed hardware channels, OFDMA dynamically assigns subsets of OFDM subcarriers to different stations per transmission opportunity. This is a hybrid: FDMA in space, dynamic assignment in time. More flexible than classic FDMA.

Professor's Picky Corner
Shannon's capacity theorem ties frequency band to throughput: C = B × log₂(1 + SNR). A narrower FDMA band B means lower maximum capacity C for that user — even if SNR is perfect. This is a direct quantitative consequence of FDMA shown in the slides' bandwidth diagram.
FDMA does not need synchronization between users (unlike TDMA which needs all stations to agree on slot timing). This is an advantage. But FDMA wastes spectrum when a user is idle — the band is reserved but unused. TDMA wastes time slots similarly. Both suffer from the same provisioning inefficiency.
Your professor may ask: "What is the key difference between TDMA and FDMA waste?" TDMA wastes time slots. FDMA wastes frequency bands. Both waste the channel when provisioned users are idle.

TDMA and FDMA: Advantages, Disadvantages, and Guard Intervals

Slide 24 — The four considerations your professor explicitly lists

📊 The Explicit Slide 24 Trade-offs

Consideration TDMA FDMA
Guaranteed transmission? ✓ Yes — each user gets a periodic slot, guaranteed ✓ Yes — each user has a dedicated band, always available
Resource waste when idle? ✗ Yes — time slot wasted if that user has nothing to send ✗ Yes — frequency band wasted if user is idle
Out-of-band mechanism needed? ✗ Yes — separate channel needed to assign slot timing & number ✗ Yes — separate channel needed to assign frequency bands
Guard intervals needed? ✗ Yes — guard times between slots prevent inter-slot interference ✗ Yes — guard bands between frequencies prevent leakage

🛡️ Guard Times (TDMA)

In TDMA, adjacent time slots are separated by guard times — brief idle periods during which no one transmits. Why? Because precise time synchronization is imperfect. If station 2's transmission doesn't end exactly when slot 2 ends, it will spill into slot 3. The guard time provides a safety margin to absorb this timing error (including propagation delay variation). Guard times are wasted channel capacity.

📻 Guard Bands (FDMA)

In FDMA, adjacent frequency channels are separated by guard bands — narrow unused frequency ranges. Why? Because real bandpass filters are not perfectly sharp-edged (the ideal "brick-wall" filter doesn't exist in hardware). A transmitter on channel 1 leaks a small amount of signal into the adjacent frequency range. The guard band ensures this leaked power doesn't interfere with channel 2's receiver. Guard bands are wasted spectrum.

📡 The Out-of-Band Mechanism Problem

Both TDMA and FDMA require an out-of-band coordination mechanism to assign resources to users. The slide notes: "Slot assignment in TDMA isn't automatic — it requires coordination over a separate signaling channel."

This is a significant operational burden. You need a second, parallel communication system (a control channel) just to manage who gets which slot or frequency. This control channel itself must be reliable (it usually uses a simpler, lower-bandwidth fixed scheme) and adds to system complexity.

Why Contention-Based Protocols Are Attractive

CSMA/CA requires no out-of-band mechanism. Stations simply sense the channel and transmit. There is no central allocator, no signaling channel, no pre-assignment. This is the core appeal of contention-based access — its simplicity and self-organization.

Professor's Picky Corner
The one advantage listed is "Users are guaranteed to be able to send bits, continuously (FDMA) or periodically (TDMA)." This guarantee is the reason TDMA/FDMA are used for real-time traffic (voice calls, which need bounded latency). CSMA cannot guarantee when a station gets access — it might back off many times. For low-latency real-time applications, provisioned access wins.
Carefully distinguish: TDMA guarantees "periodic" access (once per round), FDMA guarantees "continuous" access. For a voice call at constant bit rate, FDMA may actually be better — the station can always send without waiting for its slot. TDMA requires the station to buffer its constant-rate voice into bursts, which adds complexity and latency.
The slide says guard bands "reduce channel utilization." The exam may ask you to calculate: if 6 frequency bands of 5 MHz each are separated by 0.5 MHz guard bands, and total spectrum is X MHz, what fraction is wasted on guard bands? Be ready to compute: 5 guard bands × 0.5 MHz = 2.5 MHz wasted out of 6×5 + 5×0.5 = 32.5 MHz total → ~7.7% waste.

CDMA: Code Division Multiple Access

Slide 25 — Simultaneous access via orthogonal spreading codes

🔐 The CDMA Concept

CDMA is fundamentally different from TDMA and FDMA. Instead of dividing time or frequency, CDMA allows all users to transmit at the same time over the same frequency range simultaneously. The key question is: how does a receiver tell apart transmissions that are simultaneous and overlapping?

The answer is spreading codes — specifically, orthogonal pseudorandom codes. Each user's data bits are multiplied (spread) by a user-specific high-rate code sequence before transmission. At the receiver, the desired user's code is known, and the cross-correlation between the received signal and the desired code extracts just that user's bits while suppressing others.

🧮 CDMA Math: Orthogonality

The magic of CDMA rests on one mathematical property: orthogonality of spreading codes.

Spreading Code Orthogonality
∑ cᵢ(t) × cⱼ(t) = 0 for i ≠ j (over one chip period)
1
User A's data bit d_A = +1. Spread by code c_A = [+1, −1, +1, −1, +1, −1, +1, −1]. Transmitted: d_A × c_A.
2
User B's data bit d_B = +1. Spread by code c_B = [+1, +1, +1, +1, −1, −1, −1, −1]. Transmitted: d_B × c_B.
3
Received signal r = d_A×c_A + d_B×c_B (plus noise). Receiver for User A multiplies by c_A and sums all chips.
4
Sum(r × c_A) = d_A × Sum(c_A²) + d_B × Sum(c_B × c_A) = d_A × 8 + d_B × 0 = 8×d_A.
Due to orthogonality, User B's contribution cancels to zero. User A's data is recovered cleanly despite simultaneous transmission.

✅ CDMA Advantages

  • No wasted time slots or frequency bands.
  • No need for time synchronization between users (unlike TDMA).
  • Naturally resilient to narrowband interference (spreading dilutes the interference across wide bandwidth).
  • Secure — a receiver needs the code to decode; others see it as noise.
  • Supports soft handoff in cellular — two base stations can simultaneously decode the same user's signal.

⚠️ CDMA Challenges

  • Near-far problem: A nearby strong transmitter's signal overwhelms distant users even after code multiplication. Requires precise power control.
  • Number of simultaneous users limited by SNR degradation — each user adds noise-floor to every other user.
  • More complex receiver hardware (matched filter/correlator per user).
  • Code planning — assigning orthogonal codes requires coordination.
Professor's Picky Corner
The slide shows the "CDMA" label specifically on Alice's device in the diagram (not on Cathy's). This suggests Alice is a CDMA-capable device and Cathy is not, or that Alice is the base station. The key point is that CDMA allows Alice (CDMA) to decode a specific signal even though Cathy and Bob's signals are also present in the same channel. The "Okay!" response from the AP confirms the separation works.
CDMA in the slides is presented as a solution to the shared medium problem — multiple users, same time, same frequency, no collision from the receiver's perspective because codes are orthogonal. But codes must be truly orthogonal (or near-orthogonal). Real CDMA (e.g., IS-95/CDMA2000 cellular) uses Walsh-Hadamard codes for downlink (perfectly orthogonal) and pseudorandom long codes for uplink (not perfectly orthogonal — why power control is critical).

The Single Shared Channel Idea

Slide 27 — Why using the full spectrum as one shared channel is powerful

💡 Motivation: Removing Per-User Frequency Constraints

Instead of partitioning the spectrum into narrow channels (FDMA style) or time slots (TDMA style), what if all users could spread their transmissions across the entire available bandwidth simultaneously? This is the idea behind wideband shared channel access.

Benefit 1: No Per-User Allocation Constraints
Removing "one channel per user" means users can dynamically access any part of the spectrum when they need it. There is no need to pre-assign frequency bands or time slots. This directly eliminates the "wasted slots/bands when idle" problem of TDMA and FDMA.
Benefit 2: Robustness to Multipath Fading
In a narrow frequency band (as in FDMA), if that band happens to be in a deep multipath fade, the entire transmission fails. By spreading across 100 MHz (for example), the signal occupies many sub-bands, and while some may be faded, most will not be. The aggregate channel quality is much more stable — this is called frequency diversity.
Benefit 3: Peer-to-Peer Support
In an infrastructure-based FDMA/TDMA system, peer-to-peer communication requires going through a base station (which must relay the frame). A shared wideband channel with distributed MAC allows two nearby devices to communicate directly without a central coordinator.
Professor's Picky Corner
This slide is the conceptual bridge between provisioned protocols (TDMA/FDMA) and contention-based protocols (CSMA/CA). The "single shared channel" idea is what contention-based protocols use. The exam may ask to contrast "why is a single shared channel better than FDMA for a bursty traffic scenario?" — answer: dynamic access avoids wasted idle bands, and frequency diversity helps with fading.
OFDM (used in 802.11a/g/n/ac/ax) is the technical implementation of "spreading across wide spectrum." The 100 MHz example (20 MHz per 802.11 channel × 5 non-overlapping channels, or 80 MHz 802.11ac channels) aligns with this idea. OFDM + CSMA/CA = the combination used in modern Wi-Fi.

Concurrency versus Taking Turns

Slide 28 — The geometric intuition behind spatial reuse

🌍 Spatial Reuse: The Most Important Channel Efficiency Concept

The slide makes a point that seems obvious once stated but is profound: not all pairs of stations need to coordinate with each other. Only stations whose transmissions can interfere at a common receiver need to avoid transmitting simultaneously.

Far-Apart Links → Concurrent Transmission

If network X (transmitter B → receiver A) and network Y (transmitter D → receiver C) are so geographically far apart that B cannot hear D and A cannot hear D (and vice versa), then B and D can transmit simultaneously with no interference at either A or C. This is spatial reuse — the same frequency being used productively at the same time in different locations.

Nearby Links → Must Take Turns

If B, D, A, and C are all within range of each other, then B transmitting to A while D transmits to C will cause D's signal to arrive at A simultaneously with B's signal → collision at A. These nearby links must take turns — either via TDMA, carrier sense deferral, or RTS/CTS.

Left: spatial reuse — geographically separated pairs transmit concurrently without interference. Right: nearby nodes sharing range must coordinate.
Professor's Picky Corner
Spatial reuse is the reason why CSMA scales better than TDMA in large networks. In TDMA, all N stations in the entire network share the same round — even stations that are geographically far apart and could transmit concurrently must take turns in the same slot pool. CSMA only coordinates locally — far-apart stations independently sense their local channel idle and transmit simultaneously, achieving spatial reuse naturally.
The diagram of nearby links (slide 28 right side) shows B, D, A, C all in overlapping circles. The exam may ask: "In this topology, how many simultaneous transmissions can occur?" Answer: at most 1 (since all are within range of each other, any two simultaneous transmissions cause a collision at any receiver in the group).

When Does Carrier Sense Work Well?

Slides 29, 30 — The two extremes and the problematic middle ground

✅ Case 1: Far-Apart Pairs

When B transmits to A, and simultaneously D transmits to C, and the two pairs are so far apart that B and D cannot hear each other — carrier sense works perfectly. Neither B nor D senses the other's carrier, so neither defers. They transmit simultaneously, and since the pairs are geographically separated, no collision occurs at either A or C.

CS is not even needed here — it naturally allows concurrent transmissions because the carriers are mutually inaudible. This is the spatial reuse case.

✅ Case 2: Same Carrier-Sense Range

When B, D, A, and C are all within carrier-sense range of each other — CS also works well. B senses D's transmission (or D senses B's), defers, and they take turns. The overhead is efficiency loss (one must always wait), but correctness is maintained — no collisions.

Carrier sense uses a "thresholded correlation value" — it compares the received signal energy against a threshold. If energy exceeds threshold, the channel is declared "busy."

⚠️ Case 3: In-Between — The Hard Case

What about when the two transmitters partially overlap in carrier-sense range? This is the problematic middle ground — the source of the hidden terminal problem. Slide 30 poses: "But what about cases in between these extremes?"

In this case, B might hear D's transmission at some level, but it might be below the carrier-sense threshold — classified as idle. Yet D's signal is strong enough at C to cause a collision when B also transmits to A (which shares the range with C). The carrier-sense threshold setting is a critical design parameter:

  • High threshold: Fewer stations defer → more spatial reuse but more collisions (false "idle" readings)
  • Low threshold: More stations defer → fewer collisions but less spatial reuse (unnecessary deferral)
The Carrier-Sense Threshold Dilemma

This is a fundamental tension in CSMA design. The threshold that maximizes throughput in a specific network depends on topology, density, and traffic load — it cannot be set optimally in advance for all scenarios. This is one of the motivations for RTS/CTS, which operates on a different principle than simple carrier sense.

Professor's Picky Corner
The slide uses the phrase "thresholded correlation value" for carrier sense. This is precise. CSMA doesn't just measure energy — it correlates with a known preamble pattern to distinguish signals from noise. A very weak station might produce energy above the noise floor but below the preamble-correlation threshold. This makes CS's effective range shorter than the physical communication range — which is exactly why hidden terminals exist.
The "cases in between" question from slide 30 is left deliberately unanswered in that slide — the answer comes in slides 31–36 with the hidden terminal problem and RTS/CTS. Your professor likely finds these slides connected, and a question about one will reference the other.

The Hidden Terminal Problem

Slides 18, 31 — The most important failure mode of wireless CSMA

🔇 Definition and Scenario

The hidden terminal problem occurs when a transmitting station (C) cannot hear another transmitting station (A), even though both their signals reach and collide at a common receiver (B).

  • A transmits to B. A's signal reaches B clearly.
  • C wants to transmit to B. C performs carrier sense — but C is outside A's range. C hears nothing — channel appears idle.
  • C transmits simultaneously with A. At B, both A's and C's signals arrive at the same time → collision → both frames lost.
  • C is hidden from A (and vice versa), even though both affect B.
C cannot detect A's transmission because they are out of each other's range. Both transmit to B simultaneously → collision. Carrier sense fails to prevent this.

💡 Key Insight from Slide 31

The slide states: "Key insight: Collisions are spatially located at the receiver."

This is fundamental. The collision doesn't happen "in the air" halfway between A and C. It happens at B — specifically where two signals with comparable strength overlap at B's antenna. Neither A nor C experiences the collision at their own location. This is why:

  • The transmitters (A and C) cannot detect the collision themselves (no fate sharing).
  • Carrier sense at the transmitters is the wrong place to look.
  • The solution must involve information about the receiver's neighborhood, not just the transmitters'.
Why This Drives RTS/CTS

If collisions happen at the receiver, the solution must protect the receiver's neighborhood. RTS/CTS works by having the receiver (B) broadcast a CTS frame — this CTS propagates to all of B's neighbors (including C), silencing them for the duration of the upcoming transmission from A. This is receiver-based protection, not sender-based detection.

Professor's Picky Corner
The hidden terminal problem is symmetric: C is hidden from A, and A is hidden from C. Both are equally the "hidden terminal." The terminology "hidden" is from any transmitter's perspective — the other transmitter is hidden from you.
The problem also occurs when C is not transmitting to B but to some other node D that is near B. C's signal may still reach B and collide with A's transmission to B. C is hidden from A regardless of C's intended destination — what matters is whether C's signal physically reaches B.
Slide 18 (earlier in the deck) mentions: "RTS transmission by node A restrains all neighbors of A from transmitting" and "RTS by node B restrains all neighbors of B." Both sides matter — A's RTS protects the senders in A's range; B's CTS protects the receivers in B's range. The exam may ask specifically which frame does which job.

The Exposed Terminal Problem

Slide 32 — The symmetrically opposite inefficiency problem

🔊 Definition and Scenario

The exposed terminal problem is the opposite of the hidden terminal problem. A station unnecessarily defers its transmission because it hears a busy channel, even though its transmission would not actually cause a collision at the intended receiver.

Setup: B is transmitting to A. C wants to transmit to D (a different node, outside B's range).

  • C is within carrier-sense range of B — C hears B's transmission and concludes the channel is busy.
  • C defers its transmission to D.
  • But: Would C's transmission to D have caused a collision at A? No! — D is outside A's range. C's signal cannot reach A. There would have been no collision at A.
  • C's deferral was unnecessary — a wasted opportunity.
  • C is exposed to B's carrier sense but doesn't actually interfere with B's transmission.
C hears B transmitting to A, so C defers to D. But D is out of A's range — C→D would not have interfered with B→A. Carrier sense causes an unnecessary deferral — reducing throughput.

📌 Key Insight from Slide 32

The same insight applies: "Same insight: Collisions spatially located at receiver."

Because collisions happen at the receiver (A), what matters is whether C's signal reaches A. It doesn't. But plain carrier sense has no way to know this — C only knows B is transmitting, not where B's receiver is or whether C would interfere with it.

Note: The exposed terminal problem causes throughput reduction (missed transmission opportunities), while the hidden terminal problem causes collision (wasted transmissions and retransmissions). Both are MAC inefficiencies, but they are caused by opposite phenomena: too little awareness (hidden) vs. too much caution (exposed).
Professor's Picky Corner
The exam often tests students on which problem is "hidden" and which is "exposed." Memory trick: In the HIDDEN terminal problem, the interferer is hidden (you cannot see it coming). In the EXPOSED terminal problem, you are exposed to someone else's transmission but you don't need to care (you can transmit safely). Both are failure modes of simple CSMA.
The exposed terminal problem means CSMA is too conservative — it prevents transmissions that would have been safe. RTS/CTS partially solves this too (see deference rules in Section 20) — an exposed node that hears an RTS but NOT a CTS can proceed with its own transmission.
Consider a quiz question: "A node defers its transmission because it heard a carrier. Was this necessarily the right decision?" The answer is: "Not always — if it's an exposed terminal, the deferral was unnecessary and reduces throughput." This is the kind of nuance your professor will test.

The RTS/CTS Mechanism

Slides 15, 16, 17, 33, 34 — The complete four-frame handshake and why it solves the hidden terminal problem

📡 The Four-Frame Sequence

CSMA/CA with RTS/CTS exchanges four frames for every data packet. These frames do two things: they reserve the channel and they create "virtual carrier sense" (Network Allocation Vector, NAV) in all nearby stations.

DIFS + Backoff

1
A sends RTS (Request To Send) to B The RTS is a short control frame (only ~20 bytes in 802.11) containing the intended receiver address (B) and the duration of the upcoming data frame (k bits worth of time). All stations that hear the RTS learn: "A wants to transmit to B, the transmission will last T microseconds." They set their NAV timer for duration T and defer.
2
B replies with CTS (Clear To Send) After SIFS, B sends a CTS containing the same duration T. The CTS propagates from B outward. All stations that hear the CTS — importantly, those in B's range but NOT in A's range — set their NAV timer for T and defer. This is what silences hidden terminals like C.
3
A transmits DATA to B After SIFS, A sends the actual data frame. By this point, all neighbors of A and all neighbors of B are silenced by the NAV mechanism. No interference.
4
B sends ACK to A After SIFS, B acknowledges successful receipt of the data frame. Absence of ACK triggers retransmission with exponential backoff.

🎯 Why RTS/CTS Solves the Hidden Terminal Problem

Refer back to the A-B-C hidden terminal scenario (C is hidden from A but within B's range):

  • A sends RTS to B. C may or may not hear this (if C is hidden from A, C doesn't hear the RTS).
  • B sends CTS. C hears the CTS because C is within B's range.
  • C reads the duration field in the CTS, sets its NAV timer, and defers for that duration.
  • A transmits DATA without C interfering — collision avoided!
Virtual Carrier Sense (NAV)

Stations maintain a Network Allocation Vector (NAV) — a software timer set by the duration fields in RTS, CTS, and DATA frames. When NAV > 0, a station considers the medium busy even if it physically senses no energy (no physical carrier sense). This is virtual carrier sense. Combined with physical carrier sense (actual energy detection), a station transmits only when both indicate idle. The CTS is what makes virtual carrier sense reach C even when physical signals from A cannot.

📉 Why Small Control Frames are Key (Slide 16)

The slide notes: "Small control frames lessen the cost of collisions (when data is large)."

RTS and CTS frames are tiny (around 20 bytes each). Data frames can be thousands of bytes. The logic:

  • Without RTS/CTS: Two hidden terminals might collide during a 1500-byte data frame → 1500 bytes of transmission time wasted for each station.
  • With RTS/CTS: The two stations' RTSs might collide — but RTS is only ~20 bytes → only ~20 bytes of transmission time wasted. Then the collision is resolved and the 1500-byte data frame proceeds safely.
Collision Cost Reduction
Cost_with_RTS/CTS ≈ 2 × T_RTS vs. Cost_without ≈ T_DATA
→
At 54 Mbps: T_DATA(1500B) ≈ 222 µs, T_RTS(20B) ≈ 3 µs
RTS/CTS reduces collision cost by factor of ~37x for large frames. Well worth the overhead of the RTS+CTS exchange itself.
Professor's Picky Corner
The RTS includes the data length (k bits). Why? Because stations setting their NAV need to know how long to stay silent. They compute: NAV = time(CTS) + time(DATA) + time(ACK) + 3×SIFS. If the RTS didn't include the data size, no station could compute the correct NAV duration.
RTS/CTS is NOT always enabled in practice. For small frames, the overhead of RTS+CTS+SIFS is greater than the savings from collision avoidance. 802.11 has an RTS_THRESHOLD parameter — RTS/CTS is only used for frames larger than this threshold (default ~2346 bytes in many implementations, meaning it's effectively off by default for most traffic). This threshold tradeoff is a classic exam topic.
RTS frames themselves can collide! If two hidden terminals A and C both want to transmit to B at the same time, they might both send an RTS simultaneously → RTS collision at B → B sends no CTS → both A and C experience timeout → both apply exponential backoff → try again with different backoff intervals. This resolves the contention cheaply (RTS frames are small) without colliding on the actual data frames.

CSMA/CA Deep Dive

Slides 15, 16 — The complete DCF operation in 802.11

⚙️ DCF: Distributed Coordination Function

CSMA/CA in 802.11 is implemented as the Distributed Coordination Function (DCF) — the fundamental MAC mode that every 802.11 station must support. "Distributed" means there is no central coordinator; every station independently follows the same rules.

Complete DCF Algorithm

1
Physical + Virtual Carrier Sense Station checks: (a) Is physical energy detected? (b) Is NAV > 0? If either is true → channel busy → go to Step 2. If both idle → go to Step 3.
2
Wait Until Channel Idle Station defers while channel is busy. When it becomes idle, waits a full DIFS before considering transmission.
3
Select Random Backoff Slot Station picks a random integer N in [0, CW−1] where CW is the contention window (initially CW_min = 15 for 802.11b/g). The station counts down one slot time per idle slot.
4
Backoff Timer Countdown For each idle slot time (9 µs for 802.11g), count down by 1. If channel becomes busy during countdown → freeze timer (do not count down). Resume when channel becomes idle again after DIFS.
5
Transmit When Timer Reaches 0 Station transmits (RTS if above threshold, otherwise DATA directly). Waits for ACK (or CTS).
6
ACK Received → Success Reset CW to CW_min. Go to Step 1 for next frame.
7
No ACK (Timeout) → Collision/Error Double CW (binary exponential backoff): CW → min(2×CW, CW_max). CW_max = 1023 for 802.11b/g. Go to Step 3 with new CW. Repeat up to retry limit (default 7 retries) before dropping frame.

🔢 Binary Exponential Backoff (BEB) Deep Explanation

The contention window doubles after each collision. Why exponential? Because the optimal backoff window depends on the number of stations contending — and that number is unknown. With many stations, a small CW causes many collisions (everyone picks similar low backoff values). With few stations, a large CW wastes time (stations wait unnecessarily long).

By doubling CW after each collision, the algorithm dynamically adapts: if collisions keep occurring, the window grows, spreading out retransmissions and reducing collision probability. When traffic is light, CW stays at CW_min.

🧮 BEB Contention Window Calculation
CW after k failures = min(2^(k+4) − 1, CW_max)
k=0
First attempt: CW = 2^4 − 1 = 15 → pick random in [0, 15]
k=1
After 1st collision: CW = 2^5 − 1 = 31 → pick random in [0, 31]
k=2
After 2nd collision: CW = 63, k=3: 127, k=4: 255, k=5: 511, k=6: 1023 (CW_max)
Average backoff slots: CW/2. At CW=15: avg 7.5 slots × 9µs = 67.5µs. At CW_max=1023: avg 511.5 slots × 9µs ≈ 4.6ms average backoff.
Professor's Picky Corner
Why does the backoff timer FREEZE (not reset) when the channel becomes busy mid-countdown? Because resetting would be unfair — a station that has already counted down 10 slots would lose all that waiting if it reset. Freezing preserves the station's "progress" in the queue. This is called the "frozen backoff" mechanism.
The CW is reset to CW_min after a successful transmission, not after the channel is idle. A station that has just transmitted does not have priority over stations that have been waiting — they all start fresh with CW_min for their next frame. This prevents starvation of waiting stations.
Under heavy load, CW grows to CW_max (1023) and stays there until a successful ACK is received. If 1023 doesn't provide enough separation (e.g., 100 stations all with maxed-out CW), collisions continue — this is the saturation regime where CSMA performance degrades significantly. TDMA would not have this problem under heavy load.

Deference Rules: RTS Heard vs. CTS Heard

Slides 34, 35, 36 — The precise rules for when to defer and the exposed terminal solution

📋 Rule 1: Hearing a CTS → Always Defer

If a station hears a CTS frame, it must defer for the full duration specified in the CTS. This is because: the CTS was sent by the receiver (B). If you can hear B's CTS, you are within B's range. If you transmit now, your signal will reach B and interfere with the incoming DATA that B is about to receive. Therefore, you MUST defer.

B's CTS reaches C (hidden from A). C reads the duration, sets NAV, defers. Hidden terminal problem solved.

📋 Rule 2: Hearing an RTS But NOT a CTS → May Transmit

This is the exposed terminal fix from slides 35–36. If C hears B's RTS (B→A) but does NOT hear A's CTS in response, what does C know?

  • C knows B wants to receive from A.
  • C did NOT hear a CTS from A — meaning A is out of C's range (A is too far away).
  • Therefore: if C transmits to D (a different node), C's signal will not reach A (since A is out of C's range). No interference at A.
  • Conclusion: C can safely transmit to D despite hearing B's RTS.

The slide (35) states: "Hear RTS → No CTS → Data. So terminal C does NOT transmit to D! But COULD transmit to D!" This resolves the exposed terminal problem — C is now smart enough to know it won't interfere.

C hears B's RTS but not A's CTS (A is out of C's range). C deduces it won't interfere with A's reception, and proceeds to transmit to D concurrently. Spatial reuse restored!

🎓 The "Receiver-Based Collision Control" Insight (Slide 36)

The slide concludes with the key takeaway: "Insight: RTS/CTS provides receiver-based collision control, not just sender-based carrier sensing."

This is the deepest conceptual point of the entire lecture. Let's unpack it:

  • Pure carrier sensing is sender-based: each sender independently decides whether to transmit based on what it hears locally. It has no information about the receiver's neighborhood.
  • RTS/CTS is receiver-based: the CTS frame propagates from the receiver (B) outward, silencing all of B's neighbors. The decision about who should stay silent is grounded in the receiver's local environment — which is exactly where collisions happen.
The Philosophical Shift

Pure CSMA asks: "Can I hear anyone transmitting?" (sender-centric). RTS/CTS asks: "Is the receiver I want to reach currently busy receiving someone else's transmission?" (receiver-centric). The latter question is the right one — because collisions happen at receivers, not senders.

Professor's Picky Corner
The slides 35–36 are subtle. Slide 35 says "So terminals C do not transmit to D!" — this means in the simple CSMA case (without the exposed terminal fix), C would incorrectly defer. Slide 36 explains that with the RTS/CTS deference rule (hear RTS, no CTS → can transmit), C correctly identifies it CAN transmit. This distinction between what CSMA naively does and what RTS/CTS enables is precisely the kind of nuance your professor loves.
The deference rules create a hierarchy: (1) Hear CTS → definitely defer (receiver is occupied). (2) Hear RTS only → optional defer (sender is trying; if you can't hear the CTS, you're probably safe). (3) Hear nothing → proceed with normal CSMA/CA backoff. This three-level hierarchy is a common exam question structure.
What if C hears BOTH an RTS from B AND a CTS from A? Then C is in range of both B and A, and transmitting to D could interfere with A (which is actively sending a CTS, indicating it's about to receive data). In this case C should defer. The deference rules cover this: if CTS is heard → defer, always.

  • "In the exposed terminal problem, C unnecessarily transmits?" — C unnecessarily DEFERS. The problem is over-conservatism, not over-transmission.
  • "CDMA has no interference between users?" — ONLY if codes are perfectly orthogonal and there's no near-far problem. Power control is needed for orthogonality in practice.
  • "What does 'thresholded correlation value' mean for carrier sense?" — CS checks whether the received signal's energy (or correlation with a known preamble) exceeds a threshold. Below threshold → falsely declared idle → hidden terminal possible.

Final Professor's Picky Corner — Global Traps

These are the cross-topic traps that span the whole lecture:

The single sentence "collisions are spatially located at the receiver" connects four different sections: wireless interference (slide 7), hidden terminal (slide 31), exposed terminal (slide 32), and RTS/CTS (slides 33–36). If your exam asks "what is the key insight that motivates RTS/CTS?" — this sentence is the answer.
TDMA and FDMA are "provisioned" (pre-allocated). CSMA/CA is "on-demand" (dynamic). The word "provisioned" in the lecture title is intentional — provisioned means resources are assigned before they are needed, regardless of demand. This is fundamentally different from contention-based demand-driven access.
The lecture notes that virtual carrier sense (NAV) and physical carrier sense (energy detection) work together in 802.11. A station transmits only when BOTH indicate idle. An exam question may describe a scenario where physical CS is idle but NAV is active — the station should still defer. Virtual carrier sense is the software component; physical is the hardware component.
RTS/CTS adds overhead (4 frames instead of 2). For small data frames, this overhead exceeds the collision-avoidance benefit. This is why RTS/CTS has a threshold and is often disabled in practice. Your professor may ask: "When does RTS/CTS hurt instead of help?" — Answer: when data frames are small (shorter than the RTS_THRESHOLD), the RTS/CTS overhead consumes more channel time than it saves by preventing collisions.
The term "carrier sense" in CSMA is not sensing a "carrier wave" in the audio sense. It is detecting the physical-layer preamble or energy of another station's transmission to determine if the channel is occupied. This terminology trip-up appears occasionally in multiple choice questions.
