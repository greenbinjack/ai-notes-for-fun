Scope & Introduction to 802.11

What 802.11 covers, and why it matters

📖 What is IEEE 802.11?

IEEE 802.11 is a family of standards produced by the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) that specifies how devices communicate over a shared wireless radio channel. It is the technical backbone of what the consumer world calls Wi-Fi. The "802" prefix refers to the IEEE LAN/MAN Standards Committee, and ".11" identifies the working group responsible for wireless LANs (WLANs).

The standard operates at two layers of the OSI model: the Physical Layer (PHY), which handles how bits are converted to radio waves and back, and the Data Link Layer's MAC sublayer, which governs who gets to transmit on the shared channel and when. This lecture is focused primarily on the MAC layer.

The three major topics covered in Lecture 5 are:

  • Scope of 802.11: The range of standards and what they govern.
  • Reference Model, Architecture, and Services: How the network is structured, what the building blocks are (BSS, DS, AP), and what services the standard provides.
  • Medium Access Control (MAC) Protocol: The rules for accessing the shared radio channel fairly and efficiently without excessive collisions.
💡 Key Insight — Why a dedicated MAC is needed

In a wired Ethernet network (802.3), collision detection (CSMA/CD) is possible because a transmitting node can simultaneously listen to the wire and detect if another signal is corrupting its own transmission. In wireless, this is fundamentally impossible: when your radio is transmitting at full power, the transmitted signal drowns out any incoming signal at your own antenna. So wireless LANs must use collision avoidance (CSMA/CA), which is far more complex and introduces overhead. Understanding this core difference explains every design decision in 802.11 MAC.

02

Architecture & the Basic Service Set

BSS, DS, ESS, AP — the physical & logical structure of a WLAN

🏗️ The Basic Service Set (BSS) — The Atom of 802.11

The Basic Service Set (BSS) is the fundamental, irreducible building block of any IEEE 802.11 network. Think of it as the smallest self-contained unit. A BSS consists of a group of wireless stations (laptops, phones, IoT devices) that share the same radio channel and are governed by a single Coordination Function (CF).

Every BSS is identified by a BSSID (Basic Service Set Identifier), which is a 48-bit value. In infrastructure mode, the BSSID is the MAC address of the Access Point (AP).

The Infrastructure BSS (with an Access Point)

In the most common deployment, a BSS includes one special station called the Access Point (AP). The AP acts as a central hub: all communication between client stations passes through the AP, even if the two clients are physically next to each other. This is called infrastructure mode. The AP also connects the WLAN to the wired world through the Distribution System (DS).

The Distribution System (DS)

The DS is the backbone that interconnects multiple APs and, through a Portal, connects the wireless network to other 802.X wired LANs (like Ethernet). The DS is typically a wired Ethernet switch. The DS is what allows a laptop on one BSS to communicate with a device on a completely different BSS — the data travels wirelessly to the first AP, through the DS, to the second AP, and then wirelessly to the destination.

Extended Service Set (ESS)

When two or more BSSs are connected via a DS, the result is an Extended Service Set (ESS). From the perspective of any station, the ESS appears as a single large logical network. This is what allows you to walk around a large building with your laptop, seamlessly handing off between different APs, all appearing as the same Wi-Fi network.

Figure: 802.11 Reference Architecture — Two Infrastructure BSSs connected via DS forming an ESS, plus a standalone IBSS (ad-hoc)
🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — Architecture

Exam questions love to test whether you know the difference between architectural components:

  • A BSS is not the same as a WLAN. A WLAN can be a single BSS or an ESS (multiple BSSs linked by a DS).
  • The DS does not have to be wireless. The standard says the DS is a logical concept — it is typically implemented as a wired switch, but 802.11 does not prohibit a wireless DS.
  • The Portal is a bridge between the 802.11 DS and any 802.X wired LAN. Without the portal, wired and wireless stations cannot communicate.
  • A BSSID is NOT the SSID. SSID is the human-readable network name (like "Home_WiFi"). BSSID is the MAC address used to distinguish BSSs at the frame level.
If a professor draws an architecture diagram showing two ovals (BSSs) connected to a horizontal line, what is the horizontal line called? → It is the Distribution System (DS). The connection from the DS to a wired Ethernet LAN is through a Portal.
03

Coordination Functions: DCF & PCF

The two modes of spectrum management in IEEE 802.11

🎛️ What is a Coordination Function?

A Coordination Function (CF) is the set of rules that governs how a station gains access to the wireless medium. Think of it as a traffic controller. When multiple stations want to transmit simultaneously, the CF determines who goes first, how long they wait, and what happens when things go wrong. The CF is implemented entirely in software and hardware within each station (and the AP).

The Coordination Function resides in the MAC sublayer and is the central subject of Lecture 5.

📡 DCF — Distributed Coordination Function

The DCF is the mandatory, baseline access mechanism for 802.11. Every single station in every single BSS must implement and support DCF. It is "distributed" because there is no central controller — every station independently runs the same algorithm to decide when to transmit.

Used for: Asynchronous (best-effort) traffic — web browsing, file transfers, email — where occasional delay is acceptable.

The DCF is based on CSMA/CA (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance). This is the core of Lecture 5.

MANDATORY
ASYNCHRONOUS

⏰ PCF — Point Coordination Function

The PCF is an optional extension built on top of DCF. It is called "point" because a single station — the Point Coordinator (PC), typically the AP — centrally controls who transmits and when. It uses a polling mechanism.

Used for: Synchronous, time-bounded traffic like voice or video, where predictable delay (QoS) is required.

The PCF operates during a special Contention-Free Period (CFP), taking priority over DCF stations by using PIFS (a shorter interframe space than DIFS).

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — DCF vs PCF
  • DCF is NOT optional. This is a classic trap. Every station must support DCF. PCF is the optional one.
  • The PCF uses PIFS (PCF Interframe Space), which is shorter than DIFS (used by DCF). This shorter gap gives PCF stations higher priority — they can seize the channel before any DCF station can.
  • PCF is almost never used in practice today. It was superseded by 802.11e (EDCA/HCCA) for QoS. If asked about modern QoS in Wi-Fi, the answer is 802.11e, not PCF. But if the exam is about 802.11 as per the Lecture 5 slides, talk about PCF.
True or False: Both DCF and PCF are mandatory in every 802.11 station. → FALSE. Only DCF is mandatory. PCF is optional.

IBSS — Independent Basic Service Set

Ad-hoc mode: peer-to-peer wireless without an access point

🔗 IBSS: The Simplest 802.11 Network

An Independent Basic Service Set (IBSS) is the absolute simplest form of an 802.11 network. It consists of a minimum of two stations communicating directly with each other — no AP, no DS, no connection to any wired infrastructure. This is also called ad-hoc mode or peer-to-peer mode.

Key Characteristics of IBSS

  • No hierarchy: No station has priority over any other. There is no "master" or "central controller."
  • Distributed responsibility: Since there is no AP to act as coordinator, the responsibility for coordinating medium access is distributed equally among all stations — exactly as in DCF.
  • Only DCF is used: Because there is no AP to act as Point Coordinator, PCF cannot function in an IBSS. Only DCF applies.
  • Limited range: All stations must be within direct radio range of each other. There is no relay mechanism (unlike infrastructure mode where the AP relays traffic).

Practical Example

Imagine two engineers in a field using laptops to share a file without any Wi-Fi infrastructure. They create an IBSS. Each laptop independently runs the DCF algorithm. When Laptop A has data to send to Laptop B, it senses the channel, waits for silence plus a DIFS interval, runs its random backoff, and transmits directly.

💡 Insight — Why IBSS uses only DCF

PCF requires a Point Coordinator (typically the AP) to send polling frames to grant transmission rights to individual stations. In an IBSS, there is no AP and no designated Point Coordinator. Therefore, no station can grant "permission" to others, and the PCF mechanism simply doesn't apply. DCF's distributed, contention-based approach is the only viable option.

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — IBSS
Can PCF operate in an IBSS? → No. PCF requires a Point Coordinator (the AP role). An IBSS has no AP, so only DCF operates.
What is the minimum number of stations in an IBSS? → Two. A single station cannot form an IBSS (you need at least one other station to communicate with).

🔬 Key PHY Technologies Explained

DSSS — Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum (802.11b)

DSSS spreads the data signal across a wider frequency band than strictly necessary by multiplying it with a high-rate pseudo-random "chipping sequence" (e.g., Barker code). The receiver knows the chipping sequence and can recover the original signal. Advantage: resistance to narrowband interference. Disadvantage: lower data rates compared to OFDM.

OFDM — Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (802.11a/g/n/ac)

OFDM divides the available channel bandwidth into many narrow sub-carriers (e.g., 52 sub-carriers in 20 MHz), each carrying a portion of the data at a lower rate. The sub-carriers are mathematically "orthogonal" — they don't interfere with each other. This makes OFDM extremely resistant to multipath fading (where signal copies arrive at the receiver at slightly different times). OFDM enables high spectral efficiency and is the key reason 802.11a/g achieved 54 Mbps vs 11b's 11 Mbps.

MIMO — Multiple Input, Multiple Output (802.11n)

MIMO uses multiple antennas at both transmitter and receiver. The key insight is that in a multipath environment, multiple spatial paths exist between the antennas. MIMO exploits these spatial paths to send multiple independent data streams simultaneously (called spatial multiplexing), multiplying throughput. 802.11n supports up to 4×4 MIMO (4 antennas TX, 4 antennas RX). Frame aggregation (A-MPDU, A-MSDU) and channel bonding (40 MHz = 2×20 MHz) further boost throughput to 600 Mbps.

MU-MIMO — Multi-User MIMO (802.11ac)

Standard MIMO (Single-User MIMO) serves one device at a time, just faster. MU-MIMO allows the AP to transmit to multiple clients simultaneously on the same channel using different spatial beams (beamforming). 802.11ac Wave 2 supports 4-user downlink MU-MIMO. This dramatically improves network capacity when many devices are present.

💡 The One Thing To Remember

ALL 802.11 variants — b, a, g, n, ac — use CSMA/CA for multiple access at the MAC layer. The PHY technology (OFDM, MIMO, etc.) changes how bits are modulated over the air. But the MAC protocol — CSMA/CA with DCF — is the common denominator. The exam may ask about a specific standard's frequency or speed, but the MAC behavior is the same for all.

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — Standards
  • 802.11a uses the 5 GHz band. Despite coming before 802.11g alphabetically, 802.11a was ratified at the same time as 802.11b in 1999, but used a different band. Students often mix up a and g.
  • 802.11n is dual-band. Both 2.4 GHz AND 5 GHz. The others are single-band (except 802.11ac which is 5 GHz only).
  • The IFS timing values in the slides (9µs, 16µs, 25µs, 34µs) are all for 802.11a specifically. If an exam question asks you to calculate IFS for 802.11b, the values are different. Always confirm which standard.
  • Frame Aggregation in 802.11n is a key mechanism for reducing the overhead penalty of CSMA/CA — by bundling multiple MSDUs or MPDUs into one large transmission, the overhead (DIFS, backoff, ACK) is amortized over more data.
Which 802.11 standard introduced channel bonding? → 802.11n (40 MHz = two bonded 20 MHz channels). 802.11ac extended this to 80 and 160 MHz.
Does 802.11ac support 2.4 GHz? → No. 802.11ac operates exclusively in the 5 GHz band. Only 802.11n offers both bands.

Listen Before Talk — CCA & NAV

Physical and virtual carrier sensing mechanisms

📻 Clear Channel Assessment (CCA) — Physical Carrier Sensing

Before any station transmits, it must check whether the channel is idle. This channel sensing function is called Clear Channel Assessment (CCA). It is the "listen" in "Listen Before Talk."

How CCA Works

CCA measures the power level of the received radio signal. It compares this to a fixed threshold:

  • Power threshold: –82 dBm (this value is implementation-dependent but –82 dBm is the reference). This is an extremely small amount of power — roughly 6 picowatts — but it is still detectable by modern RF hardware.
  • If the received signal power exceeds –82 dBm → channel is declared BUSY. The station must not transmit.
  • If the received signal power is below –82 dBm → channel is declared IDLE. The station may proceed with its backoff countdown.

Variants of CCA

The slides mention "different variants of CCA." The two primary modes are:

  • Energy Detection (ED): Raw power measurement. Detects any energy above the threshold, including non-802.11 interference (like a microwave oven). Acts like "ordinary noise detection."
  • Carrier Sense (CS) / Preamble Detection: Looks for the specific 802.11 preamble in the signal. Only signals from other 802.11 devices trigger a "busy" indication. This is the "only signals from other 802.11" variant.

In practice, most chipsets implement a combination: energy detection up to some threshold, and preamble detection below it.

⏳ Network Allocation Vector (NAV) — Virtual Carrier Sensing

CCA can only detect transmissions from stations within radio range. It cannot detect ongoing transmissions between stations that are far away or blocked by obstacles (the hidden terminal problem). The NAV solves part of this problem by providing virtual carrier sensing.

How NAV Works

Every 802.11 frame carries a Duration/ID field in its MAC header. This field tells every station in range how long the current frame exchange sequence (including the response frames like ACK or CTS) will take.

When a station overhears a frame — even if that frame is not addressed to it — it reads the Duration field and sets its NAV timer to that value. The NAV is a software timer that counts down independently of the physical channel state.

The NAV Rule

A station is forbidden from initiating a new transmission as long as EITHER:

  • Its physical CCA senses the channel as busy, OR
  • Its NAV timer is nonzero (i.e., counting down).

This is the "virtual carrier sensing" aspect — a station "virtually" knows the channel is occupied even if it cannot physically hear the ongoing transmission.

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — CCA & NAV
  • NAV ≠ physical carrier sense. They are two independent mechanisms. A station can have CCA=idle but NAV>0 (because it heard a duration field earlier and the ongoing transmission is from a hidden station). In this case, the station is still forbidden to transmit.
  • NAV is triggered by the Duration field, not by the frame type. Any frame — DATA, ACK, RTS, CTS — carries a Duration field, and any overhearer will update its NAV accordingly.
  • The –82 dBm threshold is "possibly implementation dependent" — as stated in the slides. The professor may ask you to state this caveat.
A station has CCA=idle. Can it transmit immediately? → Not necessarily. It must also check NAV. If NAV > 0, it cannot transmit, even if the channel physically appears idle.

DCF Deep Dive — CSMA/CA

Why collision detection fails in wireless, and how collision avoidance works

🚫 Why Collision Detection (CSMA/CD) Doesn't Work in Wireless

In Ethernet (802.3), Collision Detection (CD) works because a station can simultaneously transmit and listen on the same wire. If its transmitted signal gets corrupted by another station's signal, it detects the voltage anomaly and stops — saving channel time.

In wireless, this is impossible for two fundamental reasons:

1
The near-far problem (transmit power dominates)

When a station is transmitting, its own transmitted signal is enormously stronger than any incoming signal from a distant station (potentially millions of times stronger in power). The transmitted signal simply drowns out any received signal at the station's own antenna. There is no way to "hear" a collision at the receiver while transmitting at full power.

2
The hidden terminal problem

Even if power weren't an issue, a station may be physically blocked or too far away from another transmitting station to sense its signal — yet both of their signals may collide at a third station (the receiver). Since Station A cannot detect Station C's transmission, it has no way to detect a collision at the receiver. Collision Detection at the transmitter would be unreliable even if hardware allowed it.

✅ CSMA/CA: The Solution

Since CD is impossible, 802.11 shifts the strategy from detecting collisions after they happen to avoiding them before they happen. This is Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance (CSMA/CA).

CSMA/CA has two key components working together:

Component 1: Listen Before Talk (CSMA part)

A station must sense the channel before transmitting. If the channel is busy (CCA detects a signal, or NAV > 0), the station defers — it waits. It does not transmit while the channel is occupied. This is the carrier sense part.

Component 2: Random Backoff (Collision Avoidance part)

Even after sensing the channel as idle for a minimum time (DIFS), a station does not immediately transmit. Instead, it waits an additional random amount of time (the backoff). By randomizing the wait time, different stations end up transmitting at different moments, dramatically reducing the probability of two stations starting at exactly the same instant.

Component 3: ARQ (Automatic Repeat reQuest)

Because collisions and channel errors can still occur, 802.11 uses link-layer acknowledgments (ACKs). After every unicast data frame, the receiver must send an ACK. If the sender does not receive an ACK within a timeout, it assumes the frame was lost and retransmits. This is Automatic Repeat reQuest (ARQ) — specifically Stop-and-Wait ARQ at the link layer. The need for ACKs stems from the high bit error rates in wireless channels due to fading, interference, and noise.

💡 Why ARQ is critical in wireless but less critical in wired

Wired Ethernet has bit error rates (BER) on the order of 10⁻¹² or better. The probability of a frame being corrupted is negligible. In wireless, BER can be as high as 10⁻³ or worse due to multipath fading, interference, and signal attenuation. For a 1000-byte frame, a BER of 10⁻³ means a per-frame error probability of about 1 – (1–10⁻³)^8000 ≈ 1 – e⁻⁸ ≈ 99.97%. Without ARQ, almost no data would be reliably delivered. With ARQ, the MAC layer handles retransmission transparently so that higher layers see a reliable channel.

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — DCF / CSMA/CA
  • CSMA/CA does NOT eliminate collisions — it only reduces their probability. Two stations can still pick the same random backoff value and collide. The exponential backoff mechanism addresses repeated collisions.
  • ACKs in 802.11 are for link-layer reliability, NOT for collision detection. The lack of ACK tells the transmitter that its frame was either lost (collision) or corrupted (channel error). It cannot distinguish between the two.
  • The hidden terminal problem is why CSMA alone is insufficient. If station A and station C both sense the channel idle (they can't hear each other), but both want to send to station B, they can still collide at B. The RTS/CTS mechanism (covered later) is the specific counter-measure.
Why does 802.11 use CSMA/CA instead of CSMA/CD? Give TWO specific reasons. → (1) A transmitting station cannot sense collisions because its own transmitted signal overwhelms any received signal. (2) The hidden terminal problem means not all stations can sense each other's transmissions, making collision detection at the transmitter unreliable.

Interframe Spaces — SIFS, PIFS, DIFS, EIFS

The timing hierarchy that creates priority levels in 802.11

⏱️ What is an Interframe Space (IFS)?

An Interframe Space (IFS) is the mandatory minimum idle time that must pass between consecutive MAC frames. No station may begin transmitting during this silence period. The IFS exists for several reasons:

  • Transceiver turnaround time: A radio cannot instantly switch from receive mode to transmit mode. A minimum settling time is needed.
  • Propagation delay accommodation: To ensure that all stations in the BSS have had time to sense the previous transmission ending.
  • Priority differentiation: By defining different IFS lengths, 802.11 creates a priority hierarchy for medium access. Shorter IFS = higher priority.

🔩 aSlotTime — The Building Block

All IFS values are derived from two fundamental constants: SIFS and aSlotTime. aSlotTime is the base unit of time used in the backoff algorithm and IFS calculations.

aSlotTime for 802.11a
aSlotTime = 9 µs
!
aSlotTime accounts for: propagation delay (round-trip within the BSS) + CCA detection time + MAC processing time + transmitter PHY delay. For 802.11b, aSlotTime = 20 µs. For 802.11g (legacy mode), aSlotTime = 20 µs.

⚡ SIFS — Short Interframe Space

Value (802.11a): 16 µs

SIFS is the shortest IFS and therefore carries the highest priority. Any frame sent after a SIFS gap will preempt any station waiting for a DIFS or PIFS gap.

When is SIFS used?

  • After receiving a DATA frame → sending the ACK
  • After receiving an RTS → sending the CTS
  • After receiving a CTS → sending DATA
  • Fragmented MSDUs — between successive fragments
  • PCF responses to polling

The logic is: SIFS is too short for CCA (carrier sense can't complete), so it is only used for immediate, known responses. The sender of an ACK knows it needs to respond — it doesn't need to compete for the channel.

📊 PIFS — PCF Interframe Space

Value (802.11a): 25 µs = SIFS + aSlotTime = 16 + 9

PIFS is used by the Point Coordinator (PCF) to gain priority channel access over regular DCF stations, but without being able to interrupt an already-ongoing SIFS-based exchange. By waiting only PIFS (less than DIFS), the PC can seize the channel before any contending DCF station (which must wait DIFS).

Formula
PIFS = SIFS + aSlotTime = 16 + 9 = 25 µs

📡 DIFS — DCF Interframe Space

Value (802.11a): 34 µs = SIFS + 2 × aSlotTime = 16 + 18

DIFS is used by all stations operating under the DCF (which is all stations, since DCF is mandatory). It is the standard wait time before a station may attempt medium access through the backoff mechanism.

Formula
DIFS = SIFS + 2 × aSlotTime = 16 + 18 = 34 µs

The extra 2 slot times (compared to PIFS) ensure that the CCA has enough time to complete and confirm the channel is truly idle before starting the backoff counter.

⚠️ EIFS — Extended Interframe Space

Value (802.11a): ≈ 200 µs

EIFS replaces DIFS when a frame has been received with an error (bad FCS). It represents the longest deferral time and therefore the lowest priority for channel access.

Why is EIFS longer? When a station receives a garbled frame, it doesn't know what's happening on the channel. It might be that hidden stations are in the middle of an exchange. By waiting a full EIFS before competing, the station gives other ongoing exchanges time to complete. Once any single subsequent frame is received correctly, the station reverts to using DIFS.

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — IFS
  • SIFS does NOT involve CCA. The slide explicitly states this. SIFS is too short for the CCA mechanism to complete. It is only used for immediate, pre-determined responses (ACK, CTS) where no channel sensing is needed because the responder already "knows" it's supposed to respond.
  • All IFS values are independent of the channel data rate. This is explicitly stated in the slides. SIFS = 16 µs at 54 Mbps and also at 6 Mbps for 802.11a. The IFS is a time domain value determined by PHY layer processing delays, not by data rate.
  • The statement "shorter IFS = higher priority" is the core concept. If you can explain WHY this creates priority (the shorter-waiting station transmits first, and once it starts, other stations sense the channel as busy and defer), you have fully understood it.
  • EIFS is ≈ 200µs — the professor may ask for the exact formula: EIFS = SIFS + ACK_TX_time + DIFS. The ACK transmission time depends on the data rate, which is why an exact universal value isn't given.
If SIFS = 16 µs and aSlotTime = 9 µs, what is DIFS? Show your work. → DIFS = SIFS + 2 × aSlotTime = 16 + 2(9) = 16 + 18 = 34 µs
Why is SIFS used before sending an ACK instead of DIFS? → Because the ACK has the highest priority — it must arrive before any other station can re-seize the channel. SIFS is so short that no other station's DIFS or PIFS can expire first, guaranteeing the ACK gets through. Also, SIFS is too short for CCA, but an ACK doesn't need CCA — the responding station knows exactly when it should respond.

Frame Exchange Timing Scenarios

The three retransmission scenarios shown in slides 13–15

📋 MSDU vs MPDU — A Quick Terminology Note

The slides talk about MSDU delivery attempts. It's important to understand the terminology:

  • MSDU (MAC Service Data Unit): The data payload handed down to the MAC layer from the upper layer (IP datagram, etc.). This is what the user wants to send.
  • MPDU (MAC Protocol Data Unit): The MSDU plus MAC headers, framing, and FCS. This is what actually gets transmitted over the air as a "frame."
  • MSDU Delivery Attempt: One attempt to deliver an MSDU by transmitting its corresponding MPDU (DATA frame).

Station 1 transmits a DATA frame. The frame is not received by Station 2 (perhaps due to a collision or deep fade). Since Station 2 never received the DATA frame, it sends no ACK. Station 1 waits for the ACK timeout, receives nothing, and concludes: the frame was lost.

Collision Avoidance & the Backoff Procedure

The mechanics of how stations avoid transmitting simultaneously

🎯 What is a Collision?

A collision occurs when two or more stations begin transmitting at the same time on the same channel. Their signals mix in the air (superposition), and the resulting composite waveform cannot be decoded by the receiver. Both frames are lost — neither is received correctly. This is the fundamental challenge that the CA mechanism addresses.

In wireless, collisions happen even when all stations are playing by the rules. Why? Because two stations can independently sense the channel as "idle" (e.g., after the DIFS period ends) at exactly the same moment and both start transmitting. The backoff's job is to reduce the probability of this synchronized timing.

🎲 The Backoff Procedure — A Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1
Sense the channel

A station with a frame to send first checks the channel using CCA and NAV. If the channel is busy, it waits and keeps monitoring.

2
Wait for DIFS after channel goes idle

Once the channel appears idle (CCA=idle AND NAV=0), the station does NOT immediately transmit. It waits a mandatory DIFS = 34 µs (for 802.11a). Only after the DIFS expires can it proceed.

3
Select a random backoff counter

The station randomly selects an integer r from [0, CW], where CW is the current Contention Window. The backoff duration is then: Backoff = r × aSlotTime. For a first-time transmission, CW = CWmin = 16 - 1 = 15 (for 802.11a), so r ∈ [0, 15].

4
Count down the backoff timer

The station counts down r slots. The countdown continues only while the channel is idle. If the channel becomes busy (another station started transmitting), the countdown freezes at its current value. Once the channel is idle again for a DIFS, the countdown resumes from where it left off.

5
Transmit when backoff reaches zero

When the backoff counter reaches zero, the station transmits its first frame (RTS, if using RTS/CTS, or directly DATA if not). It has "won" the contention.

6
Stations that lost contention "resume, not restart"

Stations that had to freeze their countdown due to step 4 resume counting down after the channel becomes idle again. They do NOT pick a new random number. This gives priority to stations that have been waiting longer — their counters are lower (closer to zero).

💡 Why "Resume, Not Restart" Creates Fairness

Imagine Station A has a backoff of 3 remaining, and Station B (which just finished transmitting) picks a new random backoff of 15. When the channel goes idle after Station B's frame, Station A only has to count down 3 more slots while Station B counts down 15. Station A wins the next contention, effectively giving priority to stations that have been waiting longer. This prevents starvation.

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — Backoff
  • The backoff countdown freezes (does not reset) when the channel goes busy. This is the most commonly misunderstood aspect. A station that has already been waiting doesn't lose its progress.
  • A station must ALWAYS perform a backoff, even if it's the only station with data to send and the channel was idle for DIFS. The standard requires a backoff after every DIFS period before initial transmission.
  • The backoff counter counts slots (9 µs each), not absolute time. This uniformity ensures that all stations have the same resolution in their random competition.
Station X has a remaining backoff of 5 slots. The channel suddenly goes busy (another station wins). What happens to Station X's backoff counter? → It freezes at 5 slots. When the channel becomes idle again (and DIFS expires), Station X resumes counting down from 5, not from a new random value.

📐 The Contention Window (CW) Formula

The Contention Window is the range from which the random backoff value is chosen: r ∈ [0, CW]. A larger CW means the station will wait longer on average before transmitting, reducing collision probability at the cost of higher latency.

📈 Binary Exponential Backoff — CW Doubling After Each Failure

After each failed transmission attempt (no ACK received = collision or error assumed), the Contention Window is doubled. This is called Binary Exponential Backoff (BEB).

The logic: If a collision occurred, it means multiple stations were competing. A wider CW spreads these stations further apart in time, making it less likely they'll collide again. The more collisions occur, the more congested the channel, and the wider the CW should be to avoid further collisions.

RTS/CTS — Request to Send / Clear to Send

Virtual channel reservation to protect long data frames

🛡️ The Core Idea: Reserve Before Transmit

The primary motivation for RTS/CTS is to protect long data frames from being destroyed by collisions. Consider: if a 1500-byte data frame collides (a rare event), the entire channel time for that long frame is wasted. With RTS/CTS, the station first sends a tiny RTS frame (only ~20 bytes). If that RTS collides, only a tiny amount of channel time is wasted — and no valuable data has been lost.

RTS/CTS effectively reserves the channel for the duration of the upcoming data exchange before the actual data is transmitted.

📜 The RTS/CTS Exchange Sequence

1
Station A: DIFS + Backoff, then send RTS

After winning the backoff contention, Station A sends a small RTS (Request To Send) frame to its intended recipient (Station B / AP). The RTS contains the Duration field: how long the entire RTS+SIFS+CTS+SIFS+DATA+SIFS+ACK sequence will take.

2
All other stations: Set NAV from RTS duration

Any station in range of Station A overhears the RTS. It reads the Duration field and sets its NAV timer accordingly. These stations now defer for the duration of the upcoming exchange — even though they cannot hear Station B's CTS or the data exchange that follows.

3
Station B: SIFS, then send CTS

Station B receives the RTS and responds after a SIFS with a CTS (Clear To Send). The CTS also contains a Duration field (slightly shorter than the RTS's Duration, by the time used for CTS transmission). The CTS is broadcast — all stations in range of B can hear it.

4
Hidden stations: Set NAV from CTS duration

A "hidden station" — one that is in range of B but NOT in range of A — cannot hear the RTS. But it CAN hear the CTS. When it reads the CTS's Duration field, it sets its NAV and defers, even though it doesn't know about Station A's upcoming data. This is the primary defense against the hidden terminal problem.

5
Station A: SIFS, then send DATA

Having received the CTS, Station A knows Station B is ready. It waits SIFS and transmits the full DATA frame. The channel is "protected" by the NAVs set in all nearby stations.

6
Station B: SIFS, then send ACK

Station B receives the DATA frame, verifies the FCS, and sends an ACK after a SIFS. The entire exchange is complete. All NAV timers near zero now, and the channel is available for new contention.

Figure: Complete RTS/CTS Frame Exchange. Note that all SIFS gaps between frames are identical (16µs for 802.11a). The NAV set by the CTS protects the channel for stations hidden from the transmitter.
💡 Key Insight — Why RTS/CTS is Often Disabled

RTS/CTS adds overhead: an RTS frame transmission + SIFS + CTS frame transmission + SIFS before any data is sent. For small frames, this overhead is disproportionately large compared to the data. For large frames (where collision cost is high), the overhead is justified. This is why 802.11 allows configuring an RTS Threshold: frames above the threshold use RTS/CTS; frames below it don't. In many real-world deployments, RTS/CTS is completely disabled ("often disabled due to its overhead" — as stated in the slides) because the hidden terminal problem is rare in typical office/home environments with good AP placement.

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — RTS/CTS
  • RTS/CTS reduces but does not eliminate collisions. Multiple stations can still attempt RTS simultaneously and collide. But RTS collisions waste far less channel time than DATA collisions.
  • The CTS is the key to solving the hidden terminal problem — not the RTS. Hidden Station C may not hear the RTS from Station A, but it WILL hear the CTS from Station B (since C is in range of B). The CTS sets C's NAV.
  • After the CTS, all subsequent frames (DATA, ACK) are separated by SIFS, not DIFS. This means no other station can interrupt the ongoing exchange using the normal DIFS-based access.
  • The slides state: "all frames... are spaced by SIFS for transceiver turnaround." The reason for SIFS between consecutive frames in an exchange is purely hardware: the radio needs ~16 µs to switch from receive mode to transmit mode.
After the RTS is sent and the CTS is received, what IFS does the transmitter wait before sending DATA? → SIFS (not DIFS, not PIFS). Within an ongoing RTS/CTS/DATA/ACK exchange, every gap is SIFS to maintain the highest priority and prevent any other station from interrupting.

The classic wireless problem, and how RTS/CTS + NAV solves it

👻 What is the Hidden Station Problem?

The hidden terminal problem arises when a station (C) is in range of a receiver (B) but NOT in range of the transmitter (A). Station C cannot detect Station A's transmission and therefore incorrectly concludes the channel is idle. If C starts transmitting to B simultaneously with A, a collision occurs at B even though both A and C each individually believed the channel to be free.

802.11 Medium Access Overhead

Quantifying the inefficiency of CSMA/CA — and the three major sources

📊 The Three Pillars of Overhead

802.11 DCF has significant inefficiencies. The slides explicitly identify three major sources:

1. Idle Channel Time During Backoff

During the backoff countdown, the channel carries zero data. Every slot that a station is counting down is a slot where nobody is transmitting. If CW = 15 (first attempt), the average random backoff is CW/2 = 7.5 slots = 7.5 × 9 µs = 67.5 µs of idle time per transmission. This is pure waste from a throughput perspective.

2. Collision Probability and Retransmissions

When two or more stations pick the same backoff value, they transmit simultaneously and collide. The entire transmission time is wasted. The CW then doubles (BEB), adding even more future backoff overhead. At 10 active nodes, the probability of at least two nodes choosing the same backoff slot exceeds 40%, as stated in the slides. This dramatically reduces throughput and increases latency.

3. Binary Exponential Backoff Under Congestion

BEB forces CW to grow as: CW = min(2^(n-1) × CWmin − 1, CWmax). In a crowded network, even successfully transmitting nodes may have elevated CWs from prior collisions. The exponential growth means a single burst of collisions can cause elevated idle time for many subsequent transmissions.

🔧 How 802.11 Can Be Improved (Slide Reference)

The slides explicitly state: "802.11 has room for improvement. How? (Will be discussed in coming lectures)." The key techniques are:

  • Frame Aggregation (802.11n): Bundle multiple MSDUs or MPDUs into one super-frame. The DIFS + backoff + ACK overhead is amortized over much more data. A single 802.11n aggregated frame can carry up to 64 MPDUs (A-MPDU) or multiple MSDUs (A-MSDU).
  • Block ACK: Instead of one ACK per MPDU, one Block ACK acknowledges an entire aggregated frame exchange.
  • 802.11e (EDCA): Replaces the single DIFS with 4 different access categories, each with different AIFSN (Arbitration IFS), CWmin, and CWmax. Voice and video traffic gets shorter IFS, winning contention more often.
  • OFDMA (802.11ax/Wi-Fi 6): Allows simultaneous transmissions to multiple users on different sub-carrier groups in the same channel, eliminating the overhead-per-station problem entirely.

  • The two highlighted inefficiency sources on slide 45 are specifically: "High channel wastage due to Collision" AND "High channel wastage due to exponential backoff." These are presented as separate but related problems.
  • At high data rates, the fixed overhead (DIFS + backoff + ACK/SIFS) dominates the channel time. At 54 Mbps, a 100-byte packet takes only ~15 µs to transmit, but DIFS alone takes 34 µs. The overhead is MORE than the data time!
Why is the overhead percentage higher at 54 Mbps than at 6 Mbps? → Because the overhead (DIFS, backoff, SIFS, ACK) is mostly time-based and fixed, while the data transmission time decreases as the data rate increases. At higher rates, data transmits faster but overhead stays the same, so overhead occupies a larger fraction of total channel time.

The Complete DCF Algorithm (Slide 40)

Sender and receiver state machines — a complete flowchart

📤 DCF Sender Algorithm (Complete)

1.1
If channel is BUSY → Freeze backoff timer

Physical CCA detects activity OR NAV > 0. The station does NOT start or advance the backoff counter. It waits silently.

1.2
If channel is IDLE for DIFS → Start random backoff timer

Once CCA=idle AND NAV=0 for at least DIFS (34 µs), the station selects a random r ∈ [0, CW] and starts the backoff countdown. The timer counts down while the channel remains idle.

2.1
Timer counts down while channel idle; freezes when busy; resumes after DIFS

This is the critical freeze-and-resume behavior. If the channel goes busy mid-countdown, freeze. When it goes idle again for DIFS, resume exactly where it left off.

2.2
When timer expires → Transmit RTS (or DATA if RTS/CTS disabled)

The RTS contains the Duration field telling all nearby stations how long the upcoming exchange will take. The "(why?)" in the slide refers to: Why send RTS instead of DATA? → To avoid wasting channel time with a large DATA collision. RTS is small, so even a collision wastes little time.

3
If no CTS received → Go back to 1.2

"(why?)" — No CTS means either (a) RTS collided, (b) receiver didn't get the RTS. Either way, the data exchange cannot proceed. The station must re-enter the DIFS+backoff phase with a doubled CW.

4
If CTS received → Wait SIFS, then transmit DATA

CTS confirms the receiver is ready and the channel is clear for the upcoming data. SIFS ensures no interruption between CTS reception and DATA transmission.

5
If no ACK → Increase CW (double), Go back to 1.2

"(why?)" — No ACK means DATA was lost (collision or channel error). CW is doubled (BEB) to reduce future collision probability. The station re-enters contention with the larger CW.

📥 DCF Receiver Algorithm (Complete)

R1
If RTS frame received OK → Return CTS after SIFS

The receiver validates the RTS (checks FCS). If valid, after exactly SIFS (16 µs), it responds with a CTS. The CTS grants permission to transmit and also carries a Duration field to set NAVs everywhere.

R2
If Data frame received OK → Return ACK after SIFS

The receiver validates the DATA frame's FCS. If valid, after SIFS it sends an ACK. The ACK is the positive confirmation that allows the sender to reset its CW and proceed. The ACK is sent after SIFS because ACK has HIGHEST priority — no other station can preempt it using DIFS/PIFS access.

💡 Why ACK is needed due to hidden terminal problem

Even with RTS/CTS, the ACK serves an important secondary function. Without the ACK, the transmitter has no way to know if its DATA was successfully received. This is especially critical because the hidden terminal problem can still affect the DATA phase in some network topologies. The ACK provides the explicit positive confirmation.

🎓 Professor's Picky Corner — The Full Algorithm

The slide asks three "(why?)" questions. Make sure you can answer all three:

  • Why transmit RTS first? → To avoid wasting channel time with a full DATA frame collision. Small RTS frames waste less time if they collide. It also solves the hidden terminal problem via CTS-triggered NAV.
  • Why go to 1.2 if no CTS? → The exchange cannot proceed without channel clearance. The station must re-compete with a doubled CW after the timeout, treating the "no CTS" as a collision signal.
  • Why increase backoff if no ACK? → No ACK signals a failed DATA delivery (lost in collision or channel error). BEB reduces the probability of the same collision recurring by spreading stations more widely in time.
In the sender algorithm, after step 4 (DATA transmitted), what are the two possible outcomes and what does the sender do in each? → (a) ACK received: success. CW resets to CWmin-1. Transmit next frame (if any) starting from step 1.2. (b) No ACK received: failure. CW doubles (BEB). Retransmit from step 1.2 with new, larger backoff range.

📐 All Formulas

IFS Formulas
PIFS = SIFS + aSlotTime
DIFS = SIFS + 2 × aSlotTime
Contention Window & Backoff
CW(n) = min( 2^(n−1) × CWmin − 1 , CWmax )
Backoff Duration = r × aSlotTime
!
n = attempt number (1,2,3,...). r = integer ∈ [0, CW]. For n=1: CW = 15 (for 802.11a). Each failed attempt doubles CW until capped at 1023.

⚡ Quick-Fire Exam Q&A

Q
Why can't 802.11 use CSMA/CD?

Two reasons: (1) Transmitted signal overwhelms received signal at own antenna. (2) Hidden terminal problem — not all collisions are detectable by the transmitter anyway.

Q
What is the backoff duration for a station on its 2nd attempt with r=5 in 802.11a?

CW = min(2^(2-1) × 16 − 1, 1024) = min(31, 1024) = 31. r=5 is valid (5 ≤ 31). Backoff = 5 × 9µs = 45 µs.

Q
Which 802.11 standard introduced MU-MIMO?

802.11ac (Wave 2). 802.11n introduced SU-MIMO (Single-User MIMO).

Q
Can Station C set its NAV from an RTS if C is hidden from the RTS transmitter?

No — C cannot receive the RTS because it's hidden (out of range). C WILL set its NAV from the CTS, because C is in range of the receiver who sends the CTS.

Q
After a successful frame exchange, what CW does a station use for the next backoff?

CWmin − 1 = 15 (for 802.11a). The CW resets to the minimum after every successful delivery.

Q
What is the PIFS in 802.11a?

PIFS = SIFS + aSlotTime = 16 + 9 = 25 µs.

Q
Are IFS values rate-dependent or rate-independent?

Rate-INDEPENDENT. All interframe spaces (SIFS, PIFS, DIFS, EIFS) are independent of the channel data rate. They are fixed time values determined by PHY layer processing delays, not by how fast data is transmitted.

🏆 FINAL Boss: The Graph-to-Math Problem

Your professor extracts math questions from tiny graph details. Here is the most likely style of question based on the backoff diagrams:

Given: 802.11a, CWmin=16, CWmax=1024, aSlotTime=9µs, DIFS=34µs. Station X is on its 4th transmission attempt. It randomly selects r=12. (a) What is CW for this attempt? (b) What is the backoff duration? (c) If the channel goes busy after 5 slots of countdown, how many slots remain when the channel becomes idle again?

ANSWER: (a) CW = min(2^(4-1)×16−1, 1024) = min(8×16−1, 1024) = min(127, 1024) = 127. (b) Backoff = 12×9µs = 108 µs. (c) Remaining = 12−5 = 7 slots. Station freezes at 7 and resumes from 7 after next DIFS.
